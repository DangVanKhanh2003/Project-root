FAQs

What is OPUS audio codec?

OPUS is a modern, open-source audio codec standardized by IETF (RFC 6716) in 2012. It combines SILK (for voice) and CELT (for music) in a hybrid design that delivers superior compression efficiency across all bitrates from 6kbps to 510kbps. OPUS is royalty-free and patent-free.

OPUS vs MP3—which is better?

OPUS significantly outperforms MP3 at all bitrates. A 96kbps OPUS file sounds better than 128kbps MP3. OPUS has lower latency, better low-bitrate quality, and hybrid voice/music optimization. MP3 has wider legacy device support, but OPUS is the future.

Why isn't OPUS as popular as MP3?

OPUS is newer (2012 vs MP3's 1993). Older devices and software lack OPUS support. However, all modern browsers, smartphones, and streaming services now support OPUS natively. Adoption is rapidly increasing for web and VoIP applications.

What bitrate should I use for OPUS?

For voice/podcasts: 32-64kbps. For music: 96-128kbps (equivalent to MP3 192-256kbps). For transparent quality: 160-192kbps. OPUS's efficiency means you can use much lower bitrates than MP3 without sacrificing perceived quality.

Can I play OPUS files on my phone?

Yes! Modern iOS (11+) and Android (5.0+) support OPUS playback natively. VLC, foobar2000, and most modern media players handle OPUS. Legacy devices from before 2015 may need conversion to MP3.

Is OPUS better than AAC or OGG Vorbis?

Yes. Listening tests show OPUS outperforms both AAC and Vorbis at equivalent bitrates, especially below 128kbps. OPUS also has lower latency than both, making it superior for streaming and real-time applications.
