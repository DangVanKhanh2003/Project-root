FAQs

What is WAV format?

WAV (Waveform Audio File Format) is an uncompressed lossless audio format developed by Microsoft and IBM. It stores audio as PCM (Pulse Code Modulation) data at 1411kbps for CD-quality (16-bit/44.1kHz). WAV files preserve 100% of the original audio without any compression or quality loss.

WAV vs MP3—which is better?

WAV is uncompressed lossless (1411kbps), while MP3 320kbps is compressed. WAV preserves 100% audio quality but creates files 4-5× larger. Use WAV for editing, mastering, or archival. Use MP3 for portable listening and storage efficiency.

Why are WAV files so large?

WAV files store every audio sample without compression—a 4-minute song is ~40MB. This size represents complete audio fidelity. MP3 320kbps achieves similar quality at 10MB through perceptual compression, but WAV is mathematically perfect.

Can I edit WAV files?

Yes! WAV is the standard format for audio editing software. Import into Audacity, Adobe Audition, Pro Tools, or any DAW. WAV supports non-destructive editing, time-stretching, pitch-shifting, and effects processing without quality degradation.

Should I convert WAV to MP3 after downloading?

Keep WAV for editing, archival, or if storage isn't a concern. Convert to MP3 320kbps for portable devices or sharing online. Many people keep WAV masters and create MP3 copies for playback.

Does WAV support metadata and album art?

Standard WAV has limited metadata support compared to MP3. Some DAWs support BWF (Broadcast Wave Format) metadata. For music libraries with tags and art, MP3 or FLAC may be more convenient than WAV.
